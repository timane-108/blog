For: MBA Sem IV Students — All Specialisations Time to read: 6 minutes Time to use the tool: 10–15 minutes

Every MBA student at some point faces the same wall. Semester IV begins, the project guide asks for a synopsis, and suddenly a two-page document feels like the hardest thing you have ever been asked to write. Not because the ideas are missing — you have plenty — but because the structure feels opaque. What exactly goes into a problem statement? How many hypotheses are too many? What does "research design" mean when you have not actually collected any data yet?

This tool was built specifically for RTM Nagpur University MBA students to answer those questions — not with a textbook explanation, but by walking you through the process live, asking you the right questions, and generating a properly structured synopsis that your supervisor can actually work with.

Here is exactly how it works.

"A good synopsis is not a summary of what you will find. It is a precise statement of what you intend to investigate and exactly how."

Before You Begin — One Thing to Understand

The tool is not a shortcut around thinking. It is a scaffold for your thinking. The moment you engage with it, you will be asked real questions — about your target population, about what you already know from reading, about which variables interest you most. Your answers shape every word of the synopsis. Two students with different answers get two completely different documents. The tool cannot do your thinking for you, and it does not try to.

What it eliminates is the formatting paralysis — the blank-page anxiety that comes from not knowing how to begin, and the hours lost wondering whether the hypothesis should come before or after the objectives.

The Five Stages

1
Choose your domain
Select your specialisation

The first screen asks you to pick your MBA specialisation — Marketing, Finance, HRM, or Operations Management. This matters because the tool surfaces a curated list of pre-approved project titles relevant to your domain, all anchored in Nagpur's economic and social context. Titles are drawn from real sectors — MIHAN, WCL, Vidarbha's orange belt, Nagpur's retail corridor, AIIMS Nagpur, the city's IT/ITeS SEZ, and more. These are not generic textbook topics.

2
Title selection
Pick a title — or bring your own

Browse the curated list for your specialisation. Every title is distinct — no two titles share both the same variable and the same target population. If your supervisor has already suggested a topic, use the "Enter my own title" option. The tool will check it for specificity — does it name a sector, a dependent variable, a geography? — and ask one clarifying question if needed before proceeding.

3
Title refinement
See two improved versions — then decide

This is the part most students find most useful. The tool presents two refined alternatives to whichever title you selected, explains briefly what changed and why, and lets you choose between the three options. Here is an example of how that looks:

Your original title
"A study of social media marketing in Nagpur"
Option A — Sharper focus
"Influence of Instagram Marketing on Brand Recall among Young Consumers in Nagpur's FMCG Sector"
Option B — Policy-linked scope
"Social Media Marketing Adoption by MSMEs in Nagpur: A Study in the Context of Digital India and Viksit Bharat 2047"

Option A narrows and sharpens — better for a clean, testable study. Option B widens scope but adds contemporary relevance — stronger for students aiming at publication or competitions. You can proceed with either alternative, or keep your original title exactly as it is.

4
Synopsis generation
The full document is built around your confirmed title

Once your title is confirmed, the tool generates your complete synopsis as running text — ready to read, copy, and share with your guide. No downloading required, no formatting to fix. The document follows the RTM Nagpur University project format precisely, covering every section your guide expects to see.

5
Policy alignment
The Viksit Bharat 2047 paragraph — added automatically

Every synopsis closes with a dedicated paragraph connecting your study to the Government of India's Viksit Bharat 2047 vision. This is not a generic paragraph that could belong to any study — it identifies which specific pillar of the vision your research speaks to (Youth & Education, Economy & Infrastructure, Governance & Technology, or Social Development), names one or two supporting schemes where relevant, and ends with a sentence on what your findings could mean specifically for a Tier-2 city like Nagpur.

What Your Completed Synopsis Contains

Every section, in order

  • Title — confirmed and refined, with your name and programme details
  • Introduction and background — contextualised for Nagpur and the relevant sector
  • Statement of the problem — the specific gap your study addresses
  • Significance of the study — academic, managerial, and policy dimensions
  • Research objectives — 4 to 5 specific, measurable objectives
  • Research hypotheses — null and alternate pairs, ready for statistical testing
  • Review of literature — key themes, foundational frameworks, and identified research gap
  • Research design — descriptive or exploratory, justified for your topic
  • Sampling plan — population, technique, and sample size with rationale
  • Data collection tools — questionnaire design rationale and secondary sources
  • Statistical tools — regression, chi-square, factor analysis, or ANOVA as relevant
  • Expected outcomes and limitations
  • Preliminary bibliography — 8 to 10 seed references to start your literature review
  • Viksit Bharat 2047 alignment — the policy connection paragraph

The Viksit Bharat 2047 Connection

India's overarching development vision — a fully developed nation by the centenary of Independence in 2047 — rests on four pillars: a thriving economy and modern infrastructure, an equitable and inclusive society, robust governance and technology adoption, and a prosperous, skilled youth. Almost every serious management research topic maps onto one or more of these pillars.

Why this paragraph matters

Examiners and research panels increasingly look for studies that connect local findings to larger national priorities. A synopsis that articulates this connection — specifically, not vaguely — signals that the student understands both the academic and the real-world significance of their work. It also makes your project more competitive if you plan to present it at conferences or submit it for publication.

A finance student studying microfinance access for women in Nagpur's peri-urban areas connects to the Social Development pillar. An HRM student studying attrition in Nagpur's IT/ITeS sector connects to the Youth & Skilled Workforce pillar. A marketing student studying EV adoption connects to both Economy and Governance pillars. The tool identifies this connection from your title — you do not need to work it out yourself.

An honest word about what the tool does not do

The synopsis the tool generates is a first draft, not a final submission. Your project guide may ask you to narrow the scope, change the methodology, or focus on a different variable. That is normal — and it is exactly what guides are for. Treat the output as a strong starting point for that conversation, not as a document to submit unchanged. The thinking the tool cannot do for you includes: visiting your field site, talking to your respondents, reading the actual literature, and arguing for your own research choices in front of your guide. That part is still yours.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use a title suggested by my supervisor that is not on the list?

Yes. Use the "Enter my own title" option. The tool will work with any title as long as it has enough specificity to build a study around. If the title is too vague, you will be asked one question to sharpen it before the tool proceeds.

What if the tool picks the wrong specialisation domain for my title?

You can correct it. If you are a Marketing student studying a topic with financial dimensions — say, pricing strategy for EVs — the tool will ask you which lens to lead with. Interdisciplinary topics are supported; you just guide the primary framing.

Is the generated synopsis plagiarism-free?

The synopsis is entirely original text generated from your specific inputs. However, you should run it through your institute's anti-plagiarism tool before final submission, as required under RTM Nagpur University's 80% originality threshold for project reports. A synopsis that is built around your chosen title and your sector will naturally score well.

Can two students produce the same synopsis?

Not if they have different titles, different target populations, or different primary variables — which is why the title bank is designed with no two identical combinations. If two students somehow select the same title, they will be prompted to differentiate by sector, geography within Nagpur, or a specific sub-group of respondents.

Does the tool write my full project report?

No. The tool generates your synopsis — the research proposal you submit before fieldwork begins. Your full project report, which you write after collecting and analysing your data, is a separate and much longer document. The synopsis is the map; the project report is the journey.
